1. Introduction 

1.1 General context 

Training a language model to distinguish between тАЬchosenтАЭ and тАЬrejectedтАЭ responses is challenging 

because the learning signal is inherently weak, noisy and highly subjective. Unlike standard supervised 

learning tasks where each example has a clear, absolute label (e.g., тАЬcorrectтАЭ or тАЬincorrectтАЭ), reward 

modeling only provides a relative preference: one answer is better than another. Rather than a clear -cut 

distinction, the task involves comparing subtle differences between two imperfect options , more like 

choosing between shades of gray than making a black -and -white decision. This means the model is not 

learning what makes a response good in an absolute sense, but only how to rank two options, which 

often differ only subtly. In many cases, both responses may be partially correct or partially flawed, and 

the тАЬchosenтАЭ one is simp ly slightly better along some dimension that is not explicitly stated [1] , [2] .

Another challenge comes from the fact that human preferences are complex and multi -dimensional. 

When annotators select a preferred response, they implicitly consider a combination of factors such as 

helpfulness, correctness, safety, politeness and clarity. However, the model receives only a single scalar 

signal indicating which response is preferred, without any decomposition of these underlying criteria. 

This compresses a rich, high -dimensional judgment into a single comparison, making it difficult for t he 

model to disentangle what specific qualities led to the preference. As a result, the model may struggle 

to generalize or may learn superficial patterns, such as favoring longer or more polite responses, rather 

than truly understanding quality [3] .

The task is further complicated by inconsistencies and subjectivity in human annotations. Different 

annotators may prefer different styles of responses , some may value detailed explanations; some may 

favor strict safety refusals, while others appreciate more helpful redirections. This introduces noise into 

the training data, making the target signal less reliable. Additionally, many examples in datasets li ke 

HH -RLHF involve nuanced or edge -case scenarios, where the difference between chosen and rejected 

responses i s subtle and context -dependent. These fine -grained distinctions are inherently harder for 

models to learn, especially when they require deeper reasoning or an understanding of social norms [4] .

1.2 Objectives and contributions 

The proposed framework implements a reward modeling approach based on human preference 

learning using the Anthropic HH -RLHF dataset. Instead of generating text directly, the model learns a 

scalar reward function that evaluates the quality of a response given a conversational context. By 

transforming multi -turn dialogues into prompt тАУresponse pairs and optimizing the model to as sign higher 

scores to preferred (chosen) responses than to rejected ones, the approach effectively captures implicit 

human judgments related to helpfulness, safety and coherence. The utility of the proposed reward model 2

lies in its ability to quantify the quality and alignment of responses with human preferences, enabling 

more reliable and controlled AI systems. By learning a scalar scoring function that reflects attributes 

such as helpfulness, safety and coherence, the m odel can systematically compare multiple candidate 

responses and select the most appropriate one for a given context. This makes it valuable in response 

ranking tasks, where multiple outputs are generated and the best one must be chosen without human 

inter vention, reducing reliance on costly human annotations by providing a scalable proxy for human 

judgment. Such model enables improved decision -making, higher -quality outputs and safer deployment 

of AI systems across domains such as education, healthcare and energy systems. 

Our work contributes to the development of more transparent and robust reward models by 

integrating (a) interpretability ; (b) feature augmentation ; and (c) bias analysis into the training pipeline. 

First, we extend standard evaluation metrics such as margins , ROC -AUC and confusion matrices toward 

a deeper interpretability framework, aiming to understand why reward models prefer certain responses. 

This includes analyzing token -level importance using techniques such as SHAP values, comparing 

model behavior across different response types (e.g., safe vs. unsafe, long vs. short) and visualizing 

decision boundari es , align ing closely w ith the goals of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI). 

Second, we propose a hybrid reward modeling approach that augments textual representations with 

four structured features. While traditional reward models rely solely on text embeddings, our method 

incorporates additional signals such as : (1) response length ; (2) refusal indicators ; (3) toxicity scores ;

and (4) prompt тАУresponse semantic similarity , enabl ing the model to capture important aspects of human 

preference more explicitly. We evaluate the effectiveness of this approach by comparing a baseline text -

only model with the proposed feature -augmented model, demonstrating measurable improvements in 

performa nce. Finally, we investigate bias in reward models trained on the HH -RLHF dataset, with a 

particular focus on bias amplification. Reward models may implicitly favor certain superficial 

characteristics, such as longer responses or more polite phrasing, rega rdless of actual content quality. 

We analyze these tendencies by examining correlations between response length and predicted reward, 

as well as their influence on model decisions. 

2. Literature review 

Reward modeling originates in the broader literature on learning objectives from human feedback 

rather than hand -coding them. Early peer -reviewed work established that reward functions can be 

inferred from pairwise preferences over trajectories, enabling a gents to optimize for goals that are easier 

for humans to compare than to specify directly. In robotics and sequential decision -making, this line 

includes active preference elicitation, batch preference querying and hybrid schemes that combine 

demonstratio ns with preferences. These studies framed reward learning as a practical solution to reward 

misspecification and showed that human supervision can be used sparingly yet effectively [5] , [6] , [7] ,

[8] . Thus, p revious papers established the basic logic that a learned reward proxy can replace manually 

specified objectives when the target behavior is difficult to formalize [9] , [10] .

A second phase of the literature expanded reward modeling beyond robotics into language 

generation and summarization. Peer -reviewed work showed that learned reward models can better 

reflect human judgments than token -level likelihood objectives or static a utomatic metrics. In particular, 

NeurIPS work on summarization from human feedback demonstrated that reward models trained on 

pairwise comparisons can become good evaluators and effective training signals for downstream policy 

optimization. This was a turning point because it moved reward modeling from embodied control into 

natural language (NL) generation and established the basic template still used today: collect preference 

data, fit a reward model, then optimize a policy against it [11] , [12] . More r ecent peer -reviewed work 

on LLM alignment has broadened reward modeling in several directions. One branch studies 

alternatives and complements to explicit reward models, such as RRHF and DPO, which clarify when 

reward modeling is necessary and when prefere nce optimization can be done more directly. Another 

branch studies heterogeneous or multi -objective preferences, arguing that scalar reward models may be 

too limited when users disagree or when alignment must trade off helpfulness, harmlessness or other 

objectives dynamically [13], [14], [15] , le ading to Bayesian preference modeling, context -conditioned 

reward mixtures and robust reward aggregation schemes [16], [17] .

A fourth and increasingly important strand focuses on evaluation, robustness and failure modes. 

RewardBench formalizes benchmark -based evaluation of reward models, while newer ICML and 

ACL/NAACL papers investigate robustness to distribution shift, noisy pr eferences, reward over -3

optimization and the value of richer supervision such as self -generated critiques [18] , [19] . Work on 

weight -averaged reward models and transformed/combined rewards reflects a growing consensus that 

raw pairwise accuracy is not enough: reward models must also be stable under optimization pressure 

and informative in downstream use. This shift from тАЬcan we train a reward model?тАЭ to тАЬcan we trust 

and evaluate one?тАЭ marks the current state of the field [20] , [21] , [22] . In the LLM setting, reward 

models are increasingly studied alongside direct preference optimization methods rather than in 

isolation, which makes benchmarking and methodological transparency especially important. Surveys 

confirm that reward modeling is now understood as part of a broader human -feedback ecosystem rather 

than a single standalone technique [23] , [24] .

A key transition came with non -peer reviewed work that applied human preference learning directly 

to language generation, showing that reward models could be trained to evaluate summaries and 

continuations more faithfully than standard token -level objectives. It helped establish reward modeling 

as a practical post -training layer for language models rather than a purely theoretical alignment device 

[25] . The modern literature on reward models is largely shaped by RLHF for LLMs . InstructGPT 

demonstrated the now -standard pipeline of supervised fine -tuning, reward -model training on ranked 

outputs and policy optimization with reinforcement learning. AnthropicтАЩs work on helpful and harmless 

assistants extended this paradigm to multi -objective alignment, while Constitutional AI further reduced 

dependence on direct human preference labels by using AI feedback guided by explicit principles ,

position ing reward mod els as intermediate layer translating human or AI judgments into trainable scalar 

objectives [26] , [27] , [28] . As the field matured, researchers began to examine whether one universal 

reward model is sufficient. Work on agreement under diverse preferences and customized reward 

modeling showed that preferences are heterogeneous across people and contexts, motivating 

personalized or group -aware reward models rather than a single generic scorer. In parallel, attribute -

based approaches such as SteerLM decomposed quality into multiple controllable dimensions, 

suggesting that future reward modeling may benefit from stru ctured and interpretable preference 

representations instead of a single undifferentiated scalar [29] , [30] .

Another major thread of the literature questions whether explicit reward models are always necessary. 

Methods such as RRHF, DPO and KTO optimize preferences more directly, often by treating language -

model probabilities as implicit reward signals or human -aware utilities [31] , [32] . Although these 

methods are sometimes described as тАЬreward -freeтАЭ they are deeply relevant to the reward -modeling 

literature because they clarify what explicit reward models contribute: modularity, reuse for reranking 

or filtering and a standalone evaluato r that can be audited independently of the policy. Theoretical work 

has also shown that many of these methods can be understood within a common preference -

optimization framework [33] , [34] .

Studies on reward hacking, reward -model underspecification and overoptimization show that high 

in -distribution preference accuracy does not necessarily imply robust downstream alignment. Ensemble 

methods and weight -averaged reward models improve robustness under distribution shift, but they do 

not fully eliminate exploitable blind spots. At the same time, benchmarks such as RewardBench and 

dedicated evaluation studies argue that reward models should be assessed not only by pairwise accuracy 

on held -out data but also by their downstream usefulness for producing bet ter aligned policies. Work 

such as Elephant in the Room further warns that poor reward -model quality can quietly limit or distort 

alignment outcomes [35] , [36] .

The literature is also expanding beyond static outcome reward models. Online and iterative RLHF 

workflows revisit reward -model training as a recurring component rather than a one -shot preprocessing 

stage. Self -rewarding and generative reward -model approaches explore whe ther strong LLMs can 

produce or explain reward judgments directly, reducing the cost of human preference collection. Active 

reward modeling extends this further by selecting which comparisons are most informative to annotate ,

indicat ing a shift from fixed binary preference classifiers toward adaptive, synthetic and more 

generative forms of reward supervision [37] , [38] , [39] , [40] . Recent surveys confirm that reward 

models remain one of the most important abstractions in post -training because they support policy 

optimization, reranking, filtering, automated evaluation and increasingly test -time or agentic control 

[41] .

3. Methodology 4

The proposed algorithm develops a reward modeling framework that learns to evaluate and rank 

responses based on human preferences. Starting from a dataset of paired dialogues containing preferred 

(chosen) and non -preferred (rejected) responses, each sample is transformed by extracting the full 

conversational context (prompt) and the final assistant reply. These elements are concatenated into a 

single input sequence, ensuring that the model captures both context and response semantics. After 

preprocessing an d filtering inconsistent samples, the inputs are tokenized and fed into a pretrained 

encoder -based language model configured to output a single scalar reward score. 

To enable efficient training, Low -Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is applied to selected attention modules, 

allowing the model to adapt to the task while updating only a small subset of parameters. The training 

process follows a pairwise preference objective, where the model learns to assign a higher reward to 

the chosen response than to the rejected one. This is achieved by optimizing the difference between the 

two scores, effectively modeling relative quality rather than absolute classification. During training, 

performance is monitored using pairwise accuracy, which reflects the proportion of correctly ranked 

response pairs. After training, the model is evaluated on a test set by computing reward scores for both 

chosen and rejected responses, deriving their difference (margin), and determining whether the 

preferred response receives a higher score. Additional evaluation metric s, including confusion matrix ,

F1 -score and ROC -AUC, are calculated to assess robustness and discriminative ability. Finally, the 

trained rew ard model can be used in inference mode to compare multiple candidate responses for a 

given prompt, selecting the one with the highest reward score , mak ing the algorithm suitable for 

applications such as response ranking, safety filtering and enhancing decision -making in AI -driven 

systems. 

Our work further contributes to the development of more transparent reward models by integrating 

interpretability, feature augmentation and bias analysis into the training pipeline (as in Figure 1) . This 

includes analyzing token -level importance using techniques such as SHAP and LIME values, 

comparing model behavior across different response types (e.g., safe vs. unsafe, long vs. short). Such 

analysis aligns closely with the goals of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI), providing insights 

into the internal decision -ma king processes of reward models beyond aggregate performance metrics.

Second, we propose a hybrid reward modeling approach that augments textual representations with 

structured features. While traditional reward models rely solely on text embeddings, our method 

incorporates additional signals such as response length, refusal indicators, toxicity scores and prompt тАУ

response semantic similarity , enabl ing the model to capture important aspects of human preference more 

explicitly. We evaluate the effectiveness of this approach by comparing a baseline text -only model with 

the proposed feature -augmented model, demonstrating measurable improvements in performa nce (e.g., 

ROC -AUC). 

Finally, we investigate bias in reward models trained on the HH -RLHF dataset, with a particular 

focus on bias amplification. Reward models may implicitly favor certain superficial characteristics, 

such as longer responses or more polite phrasing, regardles s of actual content quality. We analyze these 

tendencies by examining correlations between response length and predicted reward, as well as their 

influence on model decisions , highligh ting the presence and impact of learned biases. 5

4.2 Interpretation of the classification 

The comparison between the chosen and rejected responses reveals that the model strongly 

prioritizes supportive, safety -aligned language, as evidenced by both SHAP and LIME numerical 

contributions in Figure 4 and Figure 5 . In the chosen answer , first example (in Table 6), LIME assigns 

high weights to terms such as calm (0.0870), alcohol (0.0783), mindfulness (0.0721), stressed (0.0596) 

and breaths (0.0573), indicating that the model is influenced by language related to emotional regulation 

and coping strategies. This is further reinforced by SHAP values, where alcohol has a very strong 

positive contribution (+0.7175), followed by mindfulness (+0.2897), using (+0.2897), breaths 

(+0.2038), deep (+0.2038) and stressed (+0.1676). Even though alcohol is the most influential feature 

numerically, its impact is positive because it is framed within a corrective and explanatory context. The 

only notable negat ive SHAP contribution is emotions (тИТ0.1485), suggesting a slight counter -effect, but 

it is outweighed by the strong positive signals from coping -related terms. 

In contrast, the rejected response exhibits more conflicted contributions. LIME weights are smaller, 

with enjoying (0.0075), alcohol (0.0054), drink (0.0031) and intake (0.0031). SHAP values further 

highlight this inconsistency: while alcohol contributes positively (+0.1355) and glad (+0.0878) and ask 

(+0.0350) provide some support, several terms have negative contributions, including drink (тИТ0.1410), 

feel (тИТ0.0778) and enjoying (тИТ0.0329). These negative values indicate that the model penalizes language 

that implicitly encourages or normalizes alcohol consumption without offering guidance or alternatives. 

The numerical differences clearly show that the chosen response is driven by strong, consistent positive 

contributions (e.g., +0.7175, +0.2897, +0.2038), whereas the rejected response contains smaller, mixed 

signals with competing positive and negative effe cts (e.g., +0.1355 vs тИТ0.1410) , demonstrat ing that the 

modelтАЩs decision is not based solely on the presence of keywords like alcohol , but on how they are 

contextualized.

This analysis suggest s that each feature on its own has a relatively weak marginal effect on

5. Conclusions 

Our study addresses the fundamental challenge of reward modeling in large language models, where 

the objective is to distinguish between subtly different responses based on human preferences. Unlike 

conventional supervised learning tasks, reward modeling oper ates on inherently weak, noisy and 

subjective signals, requiring models to differentiate between nuanced тАЬshades of grayтАЭ rather than clear -

cut categories. The experimental results confirm that this complexity limits baseline performance across 

a divers e set of ten LLMs, with ROC -AUC scores remaining below 0.74 when relying solely on textual 

representations. 

To overcome these limitations, we proposed a hybrid reward modeling framework that augments 

text embeddings with structured, interpretable features, namely response length, refusal indicators, 

toxicity scores and prompt тАУresponse semantic similarity. The in tegration of these additional signals 

resulted in a consistent and significant improvement across all evaluated models, with performance 

gains ranging between 7% and 14% in ROC -AUC and pairwise accuracy. The best -performing 

configuration, based on the DeBE RTa -v3 -Large reward model, achieved an AUC score of 0.84 and a 18 

pairwise accuracy of 0.83, demonstrating the effectiveness of combining learned representations with 

domain -relevant features. 

The ablation analysis further revealed that toxicity and semantic similarity are the most informative 

individual features, while response length and refusal indicators contribute more modestly. Importantly, 

the joint inclusion of all features produces a su bstantially larger performance gain than any single 

feature alone, highlighting the importance of capturing complementary and interaction effects ,

suggest ing that human preference signals are inherently multi -dimensional and cannot be effectively 

approxima ted using text embeddings alone. 

From an interpretability perspective, the use of SHAP and LIME provided valuable insights into 

the decision -making process of reward models. The analysis demonstrated that models prioritize 

contextually framed safety, emotional support and harm -reduction l anguage, rather than simply reacting 

to isolated keywords. Moreover, the results showed that model preferences are sensitive to how content 

is expressed, favoring responses that provide constructive guidance over those that passively validate 

harmful behav iors. 

The bias analysis highlighted the presence of subtle but important tendencies in both the dataset 

and the learned models. While individual features such as length, sentiment and politeness exhibit weak 

marginal effects, their combined influence can lead to bias amplification. In particular, a slight 

preference for longer and more positively framed responses was observed, although these factors alone 

are insufficient to explain model decisions. 

Future research should explore the incorporation of richer contextual and behavioral features, 

multimodal signals and causal inference techniques to further disentangle the factors underlying human 

preference. Additionally, extending this framework to larg er and more diverse datasets, as well as 

investigating cross -domain generalization, will be essential for building more reliable and unbiased 

reward modeling systems.
