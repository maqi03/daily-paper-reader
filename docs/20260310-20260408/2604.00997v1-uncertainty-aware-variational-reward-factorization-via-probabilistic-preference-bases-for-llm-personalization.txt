## Abstract 

Reward factorization personalizes large language models (LLMs) by decom-posing rewards into shared basis functions and user-specific weights. Yet, existing methods estimate user weights from scarce data in isolation and as deterministic points, leading to inaccurate and unreliable inference. We introduce Variational Reward Factorization (VRF), an uncertainty-aware framework that represents each user’s preferences as a variational distri-bution in a shared preference space. VRF infers user distributions via a variational encoder, derives weights through Wasserstein distance match-ing with shared probabilistic bases, and downweights uncertain estimates through a variance-attenuated loss. On three benchmarks, VRF outper-forms all baselines across seen and unseen users, few-shot scenarios, and varying uncertainty levels, with gains extending to downstream alignment. 

## 1 Introduction 

Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences has attracted growing interest (Ziegler et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024). One prominent line of research is reward modeling, which first learns preferences from pairwise feedback, then guides LLM alignment (e.g., RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, conventional approaches learn a single, shared reward model that inevitably collapses toward the average user, neglecting the diverse and often conflicting preferences (Bakker et al., 2022; Casper et al., 2023; Durmus et al., 2024). Beyond this one-size-fits-all assumption, personalized alignment of LLMs has been ex-plored (Kirk et al., 2024a; Sorensen et al., 2024). A leading approach is reward factoriza-tion (Bose et al., 2025; Shenfeld et al., 2025), which decomposes a scalar reward as ru = w⊤ 

> u

ϕ,where wu ∈ ∆K−1 are user-specific weights and ϕ ∈ RK are shared basis reward functions, with each ϕk capturing a distinct preference aspect. This factorization enables efficient personalization without training a per-user reward model (Zollo et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2026). However, existing reward factorization methods face two fundamental challenges in model-ing user weights wu: (C1) isolated preference modeling and (C2) deterministic point estimates .

(C1) Inaccurate estimation from isolated preference modeling: Inferring user preferences requires sufficient observations, yet per-user preference data is typically scarce 1 (Christiano et al., 2017; Ryan et al., 2025), leading to inaccurate estimation. While users often share com-mon preference patterns that could compensate for limited data, existing methods estimate each user’s weight in isolation, failing to leverage this commonality. This problem extends to unseen users, who need to estimate wu from scratch without any learned structure. We empirically confirm that performance generally degrades with less data in our experiments.

Intuitively, the estimate for User B should entail greater uncertainty than that for User A. However, without uncertainty quantification, deterministic point estimates implicitly assign uniform certainty to every user, leading to unreliable inference of user preferences. In this paper, we propose Variational Reward Factorization ( VRF ) for uncertainty-aware LLM personalization. The core idea of VRF is to represent each user not as a deterministic point but as a variational distribution in a shared preference space, from which user-specific weights are derived. Specifically, VRF introduces three key components: (1) a variational encoder that infers each user’s preferences as a Gaussian distribution from a small reference set, (2) shared probabilistic preference bases with Wasserstein distance-based matching that jointly accounts for preference direction and confidence, and (3) a variance-attenuated preference loss that downweights uncertain reward estimates during training. VRF systematically addresses both challenges. For C1 , the shared preference bases encode population-level patterns, allowing data-scarce users to benefit from collective signals. For 

C2 , the probabilistic formulation of users and bases enables uncertainty-aware preference modeling, supported by the variance-attenuated loss. At inference, the variational encoder with shared bases enables effective user weight estimation for unseen users in a single forward pass without any optimization. Our core contributions are as follows: • We identify two fundamental challenges of existing reward factorization approaches that remain underexplored in the literature, highlighting the need for systematic investigation. • We propose VRF, an uncertainty-aware reward factorization framework that represents users and preference bases as probabilistic distributions over a shared preference space. • We validate VRF on three datasets, showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on seen and unseen users. Further analyses highlight its robustness across vary-ing data scarcity and uncertainty levels, with gains extending to downstream alignment. 

## 2 Related Work 

LLM Personalization. Personalizing LLMs to individual user preferences has been ex-plored along several axes. Prompt-based methods incorporate user histories (Dai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) or retrieval-augmented context (Richardson et al., 2023; Salemi et al., 2024) into the input without additional training. Other approaches personalize LLMs at the parametric level, including per-user adapters (Tan et al., 2024a;b), user embeddings (Doddapaneni et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025), and steering vectors (Cao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). While these methods implicitly encode user preferences at either the input or parametric level, our approach explicitly models them within the reward modeling framework. 

Personalized Reward Modeling. Reward modeling directly learns human preferences from pairwise comparisons to guide LLM alignment (e.g., RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, conventional approaches learn a single, shared reward model that fails to capture diverse user preferences (Bakker et al., 2022; Casper et al., 2023; Durmus et al., 2024). To address this, reward factorization represents the personalized reward using shared basis functions and user-specific weights (Bose et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Shenfeld et al., 2025). Yet, existing methods face two key limitations in modeling user weights: (1) isolated per-user estimation that ignores shared 2Preprint. Under review. preference patterns, and (2) deterministic point estimates that lack uncertainty quantification. Although PAL (Chen et al., 2025) employs shared prototypes, they are designed for reward computation rather than user weight estimation, and its ideal point formulation lacks uncertainty quantification. 2 VPL (Poddar et al., 2024) introduces variational inference for measuring uncertainty, but infers each user in isolation, leading to posterior collapse under sparse feedback (Kim & Kim, 2026). These challenges motivate an uncertainty-aware reward factorization that leverages shared preference patterns beyond individual observations.

3.1 Problem Formulation 

For each user u ∈ U , we partition pairwise preference data into two disjoint sets: a reference set Cu for inferring user preferences and a target set Du for computing the personalized reward. Each set contains triplets (x, y+, y−), where x ∈ X is a query and y+, y− ∈ Y

## 4 Variational Reward Factorization 

To address these challenges, we propose Variational Reward Factorization (VRF ), which represents each user’s preferences not as a deterministic point, but as a variational distribu-tion in a shared preference space, from which the user weight is derived. We first infer the user preference distribution (§4.1), then derive the user weight in probabilistic preference space (§4.2). We further propose uncertainty-aware training objectives (§4.3). Finally, we present few-shot inference for unseen users (§4.4). Figure 2 shows an overview of VRF.

At inference time, the basis reward functions ϕ are known, but the user weight wu′ for an unseen user u′ is derived on-the-fly. 7 Given a small reference set Cu′ , the variational encoder infers the user distribution qu′ (§4.1), from which wu′ is derived (§4.2). Unlike methods requiring gradient-based optimiza-tion (Bose et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Shenfeld et al., 2025), VRF estimates wu′ via a single forward pass without additional optimization.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present VRF for uncertainty-aware LLM personalization, addressing two key challenges in reward factorization. For C1 , VRF introduces shared preference bases, al-lowing data-scarce and unseen users to leverage collective preference patterns. For C2 , VRF represents both users and bases as Gaussians for uncertainty-aware distributional matching, further enhanced by variance-attenuated training. Experiments on three benchmarks show consistent improvements over all baselines, robustness to scarcity and uncertainty, and gains in downstream alignment. We believe VRF provides an effective and scalable path for reliable LLM personalization. Future directions include query-adaptive personalization across diverse contexts and temporal adaptation to evolving user preferences. 9Preprint. Under review.

## A Experimental Setup 

A.1 Datasets 

Following state-of-the-art reward factorization work (Chen et al., 2025; Bose et al., 2025; Shenfeld et al., 2025), we evaluate on three benchmarks spanning synthetic and real-world personalized preference data. Detailed statistics are provided in Table 3. • PersonalLLM (Zollo et al., 2024) provides 9,402 prompts, each with 8 candidate responses scored by 10 reward models (i.e., r ∈ R10 ). To construct personalized preference pairs, we synthesize a user preference vector w ∼ Dirichlet (α) ∈ R10 and compute a composite score s = w⊤r. For each prompt, we form a preference pair from the highest- and lowest-scoring responses. We synthesize 2,000 users, equally split into seen and unseen. We evaluate across three diversity levels, α ∈ { 0.001, 0.01, 0.1 }, where a smaller α produces more heterogeneous preferences, making personalization more critical. • TL;DR (Stiennon et al., 2020) contains pairwise human preference data over Reddit post summaries, organized by individual annotators. We retain annotators with at least 50 annotations and randomly select 40, partitioning them into 20 seen and 20 unseen users. Seen users have n ∈ { 100, 150 } triplets for training. • PRISM (Kirk et al., 2024b) collects multi-turn conversations between crowdworkers and LLMs, where annotators score multiple model responses per interaction. We use the demographically balanced subset (623 seen / 623 unseen users) and construct pairwise preference data from each interaction using the highest- and lowest-scored responses.

## B Additional Experiments 

B.1 Backbone Scaling 

Table 5 examines the effect of backbone scale by varying Qwen3 from 0.6B to 8B parameters with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuning ( r=16, α=32) under identical hyperparameters. While larger backbones consistently yield higher accuracy, the improvement comes at a significant efficiency cost (5.5× training time and 5.8× inference time for +0.7% accuracy). This accuracy–efficiency trade-off suggests that smaller backbones are a practical choice for VRF, as shared preference bases and uncertainty-aware distributional matching compensate for reduced backbone capacity.

In other words, a small number of shared preference bases suffices to capture population-level preference diversity, making VRF scalable as K remains fixed regardless of user population size. 14 Preprint. Regularization strength (β). Figure 5 (right) shows the effect of varying β ∈{0.0, 0.001, 0.01, 0.1 }. Across all three datasets, performance peaks at β=0.001 and de-grades at both extremes.
