## Abstract 

Modern recommender systems must adapt to dy-namic, need-specific objectives for diverse recom-mendation scenarios, yet most traditional recom-menders are optimized for a single static target and struggle to reconfigure behavior on demand. Recent advances in reinforcement-learning-based post-training have unlocked strong instruction-following and reasoning capabilities in LLMs, suggesting a principled route for aligning them to complex recommendation goals. Motivated by this, we study closed-set autoregressive ranking, where an LLM generates a permutation over a fixed candidate set conditioned on user context and an explicit need instruction. However, apply-ing RL to this setting faces two key obstacles: (i) sequence-level rewards yield coarse credit assign-ment that fails to provide fine-grained training signals, and (ii) interaction feedback is sparse and noisy, which together lead to inefficient and unsta-ble updates. We propose FlexRec, a post-training RL framework that addresses both issues with (1) a causally grounded item-level reward based on counterfactual swaps within the remaining candi-date pool, and (2) critic-guided, uncertainty-aware scaling that explicitly models reward uncertainty and down-weights low-confidence rewards to sta-bilize learning under sparse supervision. Across diverse recommendation scenarios and objectives, FlexRec achieves substantial gains: it improves NDCG@5 by up to 59% and Recall@5 by up to 109.4% in need-specific ranking, and further achieves up to 24.1% Recall@5 improvement un-der generalization settings, outperforming strong traditional recommenders and LLM-based base-lines.

## 1. Introduction 

Existing recommender systems are typically optimized for a single objective (e.g., click-through rate in advertising or purchases in e-commerce platforms (He & Chua, 2017; Guo et al., 2017)). While exhibiting high performance, this paradigm fails to reflect the complexity of real-world recom-mendation scenarios. In practice, user intents are inherently dynamic (Mao et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025). Meanwhile, merchants’ objectives can also shift over time, for example, between brand awareness and driving sales. The inability to flexibly adapt recommendation strategies to such evolving user and business needs remains a fundamental limitation of current recommender systems. LLMs offer a promising direction for addressing this chal-lenge. With strong instruction-following and generalization abilities (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2025b; Brown et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021), they provide a natural interface for modeling complex preferences and adapting recommenda-tion behavior through prompts or task specifications. This motivates the integration of LLMs into recommender sys-tems (Deng et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2025a; Zhang, 2023), potentially enabling dynamic control over recommendation objectives without retraining task-specific models. However, because LLMs are predominantly trained on large-scale general-purpose text corpora, they remain weakly aligned with recommendation tasks and cannot be directly deployed as recommenders without further adaptation. Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) (Lambert et al., 2024) has recently emerged as an effective paradigm for aligning LLMs with downstream objectives. Recent work has begun applying RLVR to LLM-based rec-ommendation. Rec-R1 (Lin et al., 2025b) proposes a gen-eral framework that optimizes an LLM with sequence-level feedback from downstream recommendation models using GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), while ConvRec-R1 (Zhu et al., 2025b) further explores rank-level GRPO for conversational recommendation. However, adopting GRPO for dynamic recommendation introduces several fundamental challenges. First, interaction annotations are inherently sparse. In realistic scenarios, only a small subset of items receives explicit feedback from the user, while the majority remain unlabeled. Existing work 1

therefore often relies on learned interaction predictors to complete missing supervision (Yang et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a), but these estimates inevitably contain error and can vary substantially in reliability. This is especially problematic in RL, where high-variance reward estimates can strongly distort advantage estimation and destabilize policy updates (Zhu et al., 2025a). Second, existing RL methods for LLM-based recommenda-tion systems typically operate with sequence-level rewards (Shao et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025), assign-ing a single scalar value to the entire ranked list. However, recommendation is a structured decision process in which the model constructs a ranking through a sequence of atomic item placements. A single list-level reward therefore fails to capture which atomic ranking decisions are beneficial or harmful, obscuring fine-grained credit assignment despite the richer supervision inherently available in ranked outputs. Although some prior work (Zhu et al., 2025b) explores item-level supervision, the reward design makes item-level rewards not directly comparable across items, leading to unstable updates. To address these challenges, we propose FlexRec, a princi-pled post-training framework for aligning LLM-based rec-ommender systems with dynamic recommendation strate-gies. Our key observation is that RL for recommendation must simultaneously solve two problems: fine-grained credit assignment over a ranked list and robust optimiza-tion under sparse, noisy reward completion. Accordingly, our approach makes two key contributions: • Uncertainty-guided GRPO. To address the challenge of sparse and noisy reward completion, we train a critic that jointly predicts reward values and their uncertainty, and integrate this confidence signal directly into GRPO updates to down-weight unreliable reward estimates. • Swap-based item-level reward. To address the chal-lenge of coarse sequence-level supervision, we intro-duce a counterfactual swap operation within the re-maining candidate pool to evaluate the marginal con-tribution of each item placement, producing dense, position-aware supervision for autoregressive ranking. Together, these components enable stable and effective align-ment of LLMs for dynamic, need-aware recommendation. Our experiments across diverse recommendation scenarios and user needs: including maximizing interest, encouraging exploration, and promoting trending items, demonstrate that FlexRec effectively aligns LLMs as strong recommenders, outperforming both traditional and LLM-based baselines. We further show that the trained LLM recommender gen-eralizes efficiently to unseen needs. Finally, a single LLM trained jointly on all needs remains competitive across all scenarios, dynamically adapting its reasoning and ranking strategy, making it practical as a universal recommender. 

## 2. Related Work 

LLMs for Multi-Need Recommendation Recommender systems have shifted from traditional collaborative filtering and ID-based sequential encoders (e.g., BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019) and S3-Rec (Zhou et al., 2020)) toward gener-ative paradigms built on Large Language Models (LLMs). Current approaches typically rely on instruction-tuning (Deng et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2025b; Rajput et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) or in-context learning (Lyu et al., 2024) to leverage LLMs’ semantic reasoning and world knowledge for modeling user behavior. However, most LLM-based recommenders remain static evaluators of historical data (Wu et al., 2018; Dhelim et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024; Zhang, 2023; Yang et al., 2023a): they optimize for a single implicit objective and can struggle when a user’s immediate, real-time requirements deviate from their long-term profile. This motivates recommendation models that can condition on explicit need specifications and adapt their behavior accord-ingly. In this work, we take a step toward multi-need LLM recommendation by contributing a suite of need-specific instruction-tuning datasets tailored for LLM post-training, and by proposing FlexRec, which adapts to flexible user needs at inference. 

Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards Re-inforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has significantly enhanced LLM reasoning in deterministic do-mains such as mathematics (Shao et al., 2024), coding (Chen, 2021), and open-domain tasks (Ma et al., 2025). Recent RLVR-based post training methods for recommenda-tion like Rec-R1 use sequence-level metrics such as NDCG (Lin et al., 2025b) to guide model training. While effec-tive, this design assigns a single advantage to all tokens in a rollout, yielding coarse credit assignment that cannot distinguish the contributions of individual items. ConvRec-R1 Zhu et al. (2025b) use a rank-level reward; however, in autoregressive ranking the reward at a given rank depends on the prefix and remaining candidate pool, so it is not comparable across rollouts and can bias credit assignment. In contrast, FlexRec introduces a swap-based item-level reward that respects the fundamental properties of autore-gressive ranking to enable fine-grained updates, leading to faster convergence and better performance. 

Sparse User Interactions Data sparsity poses a major challenge in recommendation: most user–item pairs are unobserved, making RLVR difficult due to limited reward supervision. A common remedy is to train a lightweight neural critic to impute rewards for unobserved pairs (Lo-bel et al., 2020; He et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2017; He & Chua, 2017), but proxy errors are particularly harmful for LLM post-training since erroneous advantage (magnitude 2FlexRec: Adapting LLM-based Recommenders for Flexible Needs via Reinforcement Learning 

and sign) directly drives policy updates. To mitigate this, FlexRec augments the critic to predict both value and un-certainty, and incorporates uncertainty into the update to down-weight unreliable estimates and stabilize training. 

## 3. Method 

In this section, we present FlexRec, an RL post-training framework that trains LLM recommenders via item-level GRPO and uncertainty-aware updates under sparse feed-back. 

3.1. Problem Formulation: Need-specific Autoregressive Ranking 

We study need-specific ranking, where the model outputs an ordered list optimized for a given need, and each need induces a relevance function over the candidate set. We rep-resent both context and candidate items in natural language and produce rankings via text generation, enabling LLMs to reason over heterogeneous signals (e.g., user history, item metadata, and need instructions) and to transfer more easily to new needs without relying on task-specific retraining. Let x ≜ (U, C, M ) denote the context, where U is user information (e.g., history), C is the candidate set and M is metadata/task instruction. Let n ∈ N denote the need (e.g., engagement, niche discovery, trend promotion). Given a candidate set C, a language model induces a need-conditioned ranking policy by generating an ordered se-quence over C (with optional reasoning) 

y = ( a1, . . . , a K ), ak ∈ C \ { a1, . . . , a k−1},

where at each step k it selects one item from the remaining candidate pool. This defines an autoregressive decision process πθ (ak | a<k , x, n ) which maps the context, the target need, and the previously selected items a<k to a distribution over the remaining candidates. The quality of a generated ranking y is evaluated by a need-specific objective Rn(y; x), which is decomposable over items:

3.2. Swap-based Item-level Reward 

The primary limitations of prior RL-based LLM recom-menders are reward designs that are either overly coarse or biased: sequence-level GRPO like Rec-R1 (Lin et al., 2025b) assigns a single advantage to all tokens, while per-position gains used in ConvRec-R1 (Zhu et al., 2025b) de-pend on the prefix and remaining candidate pool, making item-level signals incomparable across rollouts and prone to inaccurate credit assignment. 

Properties of Autoregressive Reranking. To address these limitations, we formalize three fundamental proper-ties of autoregressive ranking with language models. These insights characterize the structure of the ranking problem and guide the design of correct item-level reward signals. 

Insight 1: Causality. The contribution of ak is defined only relative to the remaining pool Ck = C \ { a1, . . . , a k−1},because earlier-ranked items are already fixed and cannot be affected by the choice of ak. Therefore, any valid credit signal for ak should depend on how it compares to items in 

Ck, rather than on its absolute contribution alone. 

Insight 2: Swaps as Atomic Actions. Let C =

y(k↔j) ≜ τk,j (y).

The set {τk,j }k<j generates SK , i.e., any permutation can be obtained by composing pairwise swaps. We therefore view a single swap as an atomic local edit of a ranking, and attribute local changes in listwise quality to swapping. 

Insight 3: Marginal Contribution via Counterfactual Relocation. Item-level credit should be defined through counterfactual rankings in which the position of an item is changed while the rest of the list is held fixed. Using the swap operator from insight 2, the natural local coun-terfactual for the item at rank k is to exchange it with a lower-ranked item j > k , yielding y(k↔j). 3.3. Uncertainty-Aware GRPO 

As discussed above, sparsity in user–item interactions makes reward imputation in RLVR inherently difficult, so prior work often relies on a critic, typically a lightweight neural model, to predict missing rewards. However, with limited supervision, critic estimates are noisy and often miscali-brated, leading to unstable updates when used for training. To address this challenge, we propose an uncertainty-aware GRPO that down-weights unreliable rewards, preventing spurious estimates from misleading policy updates. Concretely, we train a neural critic to jointly predict user–item interaction rewards and their uncertainty (see Appendix G for architecture and training details). Given context x and a candidate item ak, the critic outputs a mean reward estimate ˆrn(ak; x) together with a predictive vari-ance Var[ rn(ak; x)] , where larger variance indicates less reliable reward estimation. Empirically, using critic-predicted rewards yields consis-tent gains over simple collaborative signals in our ablations (Sec. 8), underscoring the importance of a learned critic under sparse user–item interactions.

## 4. Experiments 

In this section, we describe the datasets used in our work and how we construct a diverse suite of needs. We then show that FlexRec is both an efficient learner when trained for a single need and a strong generalist that transfers effectively across needs, enabling a universal LLM ranker (see Appendix C for training details). 

4.1. Datasets 

In this work we focus on the following datasets: KuaiRec, ML-1M and ESCI. • KuaiRec (Gao et al., 2022). A fully observed short-video recommendation dataset with dense user–item interac-tions and rich user/video metadata; to better reflect sparse implicit feedback, we uniformly subsample 10% interac-tions per user. • MovieLens (Umemoto, 2022). A classic rating-based benchmark capturing long-term user preferences over movies. • ESCI (Reddy et al., 2022). A product-search benchmark where the model ranks a fixed set of items relevant to a textual query. Together, these benchmarks span a wide range of recommen-dation scenarios, from short-form content consumption and long-horizon preference modeling to query-driven ranking. 

Implementation details. For details of dataset construction refer to Appendix A. 

4.2. Need-Specific Objective Design 

To address the lack of public benchmarks that explicitly an-notate user needs in recommendation, we construct a suite 5FlexRec: Adapting LLM-based Recommenders for Flexible Needs via Reinforcement Learning 

of need-specific objectives on top of each dataset. These needs span realistic scenarios, from maximizing user inter-est, to surfacing new topics a user is likely to appreciate, to promoting items that are trending on the platform. For details of label construction see Appendix B 

Maximizing Interest Ranks items by user preference sig-nals, so items the user is most likely to enjoy and engage with receive the highest scores. 

Explore New Topics Prioritizes niche or underexposed items that match the user’s interests but come from topic-s/genres the user has not recently interacted with, so items with strong interest alignment and low historical exposure are scored highest. 

Trend Promotion Balances personalization with temporal popularity, so items that are both relevant to the user and highly popular in the recent time window (e.g., the past day) receive the highest scores. 

4.3. Evaluation Metrics We evaluate the generated rankings using NDCG at K ∈ { 5, 10 , 30 } (denoted N@5, N@10, and N@30), along with Recall@5 and MRR@5. These metrics jointly capture overall listwise ranking quality and the quality of the top-ranked items, which is a primary concern in prac-tical recommendation systems. For more details regarding evaluation see Appendix F.

4.6. Ablation Studies Item-level Reward Formulation Table 7 shows the perfor-mance of different reward constructions under both GRPO and PPO. Independent contribution uses an item’s NDCG contribution as reward and is independent of the current candidate pool. Swap-based contribution (non-causal) com-putes rewards via counterfactual swaps, but swaps with both previously predicted (past) and unpredicted (future) items, rather than restricting swaps to the remaining unpredicted pool. Across RL algorithms and reward formulations, our causally grounded swap-based reward yields substantial gains, offering an efficient dense reward for LLM ranking.

## 5. Conclusion 

In this work, we propose FlexRec, an RL post-training framework that adapts large language models to diverse and flexible recommendation needs. By combining a swap-based item-level reward with a variance-aware update, FlexRec mitigates data sparsity and improves the efficiency of credit assignment beyond sequence-level training. Across multiple datasets and needs, FlexRec achieves state-of-the-art performance, highlighting its promise as a universal, general-purpose LLM recommender. 

Limitations. We study closed-set reranking with prede-fined candidate pools and labels derived from existing sig-nals, which abstracts away key components of real-world recommenders (e.g., retrieval and open-world item dy-namics). Extending this paradigm to more general set-tings—such as retrieval-augmented personalization and ex-plicitly modeling larger, evolving item spaces—is an impor-tant direction for future work. 8FlexRec: Adapting LLM-based Recommenders for Flexible Needs via Reinforcement Learning

## A. Dataset Details 

Data Subsampling To ensure scalable training and evaluation, and to facilitate future extensions across datasets and tasks, we subsample each training set to 5,000 instances and each test set to 1,000 instances for all datasets and recommendation settings. 

KuaiRec (short-video recommendation). KuaiRec is a fully observed short-video recommendation dataset with dense user–item interactions and rich user/video metadata. Since the raw data is near-dense, we induce sparsity to better match realistic implicit-feedback regimes by uniformly subsampling 10% of interactions per user. We then construct user-level splits with an 80%/10%/10% train/validation/test partition. For each split, we build sequential ranking instances by conditioning on a user’s most recent H=10 historical items and asking the model to rank a candidate set of C=30 items; the candidate construction procedure is described in Appendix D. 

MovieLens (rating-based recommendation). MovieLens is a classic benchmark for rating-based recommendation that captures long-term user preferences over movies. We use the same dataset splitting and processing protocol as used in KuaiRec. 

ESCI (product search). ESCI represents product search, where the goal is to retrieve items most relevant to a textual query rather than a user’s interaction sequence. We follow the dataset’s publicly released train–test split for training and evaluation. 3

## B. Need Construction 

B.1. Maximizing Interest 

For KuaiRec, following prior work (Gao et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024), we use the watch ratio—defined as the ratio between the total watch time and the video length—as a proxy for user interest. Watch ratio provides a continuous, fine-grained signal that reflects both user engagement and early abandonment behavior, and is commonly adopted as an implicit relevance measure in short-video recommendation settings. We directly use the watch ratio value as the relevance score when evaluating item utility. For ML-1M, explicit user feedback is available in the form of discrete ratings ranging from 1 to 5. We treat higher ratings as stronger expressions of user interest and transform ratings into relevance scores via the exponential mapping 2rating − 1.We use the following instruction across datasets: 

> Need: Maximizing Interest

Rank them to maximize the user’s expected watch ratio (likelihood of long/complete viewing) based on their his-tory—because higher watch ratio signals stronger engagement and improves retention. Also keep in mind to recommend items that strongly align with the user’s known interests. 

B.2. Relevant Novelty 

Niche discovery aims to recommend items that expose users to previously unseen topical items while remaining relevant to their interests. In KuaiRec, we implement niche discovery using video topic tags. For a given user history, we identify the set of topics the user has previously interacted with, and label a candidate video as niche if its topic does not appear in this history. Candidates without valid topic annotations are treated as non-niche. To construct a ground-truth ranking that balances relevance and novelty, we start from a base relevance score obtained via embedding-based retrieval. We then apply a multiplicative niche bonus to candidates labeled as niche, 

score niche (v) = score base (v) · (1 + α),

where α > 0 controls the strength of the novelty preference. The resulting scores induce a ranking that prioritizes novel-topic items while preserving relevance among both niche and non-niche candidates. This formulation explicitly counteracts exposure bias by amplifying under-explored items without discarding similarity-based relevance.

## C. RL Training Details 

We perform reinforcement learning (RL) post-training on 4 × NVIDIA A100 GPUs using the Verl 4 library. Unless otherwise specified, all experiments use full-parameter training with learning rate 1 × 10 −6.To stabilize optimization and constrain policy drift, we include a KL regularization term against a reference policy with KL loss coefficient 0.01 . We additionally apply entropy regularization to encourage exploration and avoid premature collapse, using entropy coefficient 0.005 .For rollout generation, we use vLLM as the inference backend to efficiently sample sequences during RL training, improving throughput and reducing end-to-end training latency. 

## D. Candidate Generation 

We construct the candidate set using a fixed semantic retriever following STAR (Lee et al., 2025), a state-of-the-art training-free retrieval framework based on large language model embeddings. Each item is first converted into a natural-language description using its available metadata, and embedded using the text-embedding-004 model from the Gemini embedding family (Lee et al., 2024). Given a user interaction history of length H = 10 , candidate items are retrieved by cosine similarity in the embedding space. To account for temporal preference dynamics, we form a query embedding as a temporally discounted average of historical item embeddings, optionally weighted by interaction strength (e.g., ratings or watch ratios), following the original STAR formulation. The candidate set consists of the top-K most similar items, excluding those already interacted with by the user. The retriever is kept frozen throughout training and evaluation. This setup reflects realistic recommendation pipelines, where candidate generation is typically performed by a pre-trained, independently maintained retrieval system, while downstream ranking models are optimized on a fixed candidate pool.

## F. Evaluation Details 

Addressing Invalid Output Prior work (Ren & Sutherland, 2025; Deng et al., 2025) shows that negative advantage can induce invalid outputs by “squeezing” the policy out of valid regimes. We observe a similar trend: as models improve ranking quality, they may also emit invalid item IDs. We view this as expected behavior: the model should avoid low-quality items. Thus, we treat invalid IDs as low-confidence predictions. For fair evaluation, when an output contains fewer than K

Task Definition and Challenges Given a user’s interaction context which consists of historical items and observed engagement signals, we aim to predict the user’s potential interaction with a candidate item. This prediction task is inherently challenging, subsuming the reranking problem considered in this work. Accordingly, the goal of the critic is not to produce perfectly accurate engagement predictions, but rather to extract and provide useful learning signals that can be exploited during the reinforcement learning stage. In recommendation settings, such engagement signals (e.g., watch ratio or dwell time) exhibit two properties. First, they are 

long-tailed , with a small fraction of interactions accounting for a disproportionate share of total engagement. Second, they are heteroscedastic : the intrinsic noise of user feedback varies substantially across contexts, users, and items. As a result, both the expected engagement level and the uncertainty of this estimate depend on the input context. 

Critic Architecture We employ a two-tower MLP-based critic (see 5 for details) that encodes user history and candidate items into a shared embedding space, followed by a lightweight interaction network. The critic outputs both a predicted mean engagement ˆμ(X) and an input-dependent variance σ2(X). The architecture is intentionally simple, as the critic serves as a training signal rather than a primary ranking model.
