# ABSTRACT 

Recent alignment work on Large Language Models (LLMs) suggests preference optimization can improve reasoning by shifting probability mass toward better so-lutions. We test this claim in a resource-constrained setting by applying GRPO with LoRA to SLMs (0.5B–3B) for math reasoning on GSM8K and MATH datasets with difficulty-stratified analyses. As problem difficulty increases, ac-curacy plateaus, revealing a capacity boundary: GRPO primarily reshapes output preferences without reliably improving hardest-tier solving. Consistent with this, training GRPO only on lower-difficulty problems matches full-dataset accuracy across difficulty tiers while using only ∼ 45% training steps, indicating dimin-ishing returns from harder samples in this regime. We also find a cross-dataset generalization effect: GSM8K-trained GRPO achieves higher accuracy on the nu-meric subset of MATH than MATH-trained GRPO, exceeding it by ∼ 5% at 1.5B and by ∼ 3% at 3B. We show that the best achievable gains depend strongly on the base model’s prior reasoning competence and the dataset’s difficulty profile.

# 3 METHODOLOGY 

Training Framework We adopt a two-stage training protocol. Models Qwen 2.5 0.5B, 1.5B, 3B Qwen et al. (2025) first undergo a brief phase of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to establish instruction-following behavior and baseline reasoning capabilities. Subsequently, we align these 2I Can’t Believe It’s Not Better Workshop @ ICLR 2026 SFT-initialized models using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) via the TRL library von Werra et al. (2020). To ensure parameter efficiency, we apply Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to all linear projection layers utilizing the PEFT library Mangrulkar et al. (2022). Detailed hyperparame-ters for both SFT and GRPO stages are provided in Appendix B. 

Difficulty-Aware Reward Modeling We design a composite reward function that enforces both strict XML-based formatting and reasoning correctness. A key component of our methodology for the MATH dataset is difficulty scaling: we dynamically weight correctness rewards based on the problem’s difficulty level. This ensures that successful reasoning on complex tasks provides a stronger optimization signal. 

Dataset & Stratification We utilize the GSM8K dataset and a targeted selection of the Hendrycks MATH benchmark, specifically focusing on four core domains: algebra , counting and probability ,

geometry , and number theory . To verify our hypothesis regarding capacity boundaries, we map these into a unified two-tier complexity hierarchy: • Low Difficulty: GSM8K (grade-school arithmetic), MATH Levels 1–3. • High Difficulty: MATH Levels 4–5. 

Evaluation Protocol We evaluate all models using greedy decoding ( T = 0 ) to assess determin-istic reasoning capabilities. We benchmark performance improvements directly against the SFT-tuned baselines (the model state prior to GRPO alignment). Our critical analysis focuses on up-ward transfer : measuring whether models trained exclusively on ”Low” data partitions can gen-eralize to ”High” difficulty test samples without direct exposure to them. Additionally, we extend this evaluation to both GSM8K-trained and MATH-trained models, testing them on the numerical-answer subset of the full MATH test set (see Appendix B.2). 

# 4 EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS 

4.1 THE CAPACITY BOUNDARY : S ATURATION ON HIGH -D IFFICULTY TASKS 

We investigate whether exposing small models to high-difficulty reasoning paths improves general performance. Figure 1 compares models tuned on the full MATH dataset (Levels 1–5) versus the easier subset (Levels 1–3). We observe that including high-difficulty problems yields no significant gain across any model size. In fact, for the 0.5B and 1.5B models, the inclusion of Level 4–5 data proves detrimental: the 0.5B model achieves 21.5% accuracy on the L1–L3 subset but drops to 

18.7% when trained on the full dataset. Similarly, the 1.5B model sees a regression from 38.4% to 36.6% with the addition of harder tasks. Even for the 3B model, performance saturates, showing negligible difference between conditions (46.9% vs. 47.0%). Crucially, to verify that this saturation is not merely an optimization artifact of parameter-efficient fine-tuning, we conducted a full fine-tuning (FFT) ablation on the 0.5B model. The FFT results mirrored the LoRA findings, showing a similar trend of performance when high-difficulty samples were included (detailed in Appendix F). This confirms a strict capacity boundary where small models cannot effectively leverage rewards from problems beyond their reasoning horizon. As detailed in Appendix C Figure 4 and Figure 5, we observe distinct failure mechanisms dictated by model scale. The 0.5B model simply lacks the capacity to sustain complex inference; it fails to reason deeply, with its average generation length plateauing at approximately 330 tokens for Level 5 problems. In contrast, the 1.5B and 3B models actively attempt to solve these tasks, scaling their chain-of-thought to nearly 400 tokens for Level 5 problems. However, this extended reasoning leads to token overflow : the models frequently ”get lost” in their own convoluted reasoning traces or hit length constraints before a final answer can be extracted (See Appendix H for extended review). 4.2 DIMINISHING RETURNS : H ARD DATA AS COMPUTATIONAL INEFFICIENCY

# 5 DISCUSSION & L IMITATIONS 

Interpretation Our results suggest that for Small Language Models (SLMs), the relationship be-tween training data difficulty and alignment gain is not linear, but rather governed by a signal-to-noise ratio. We hypothesize that ”hard” problems require multi-hop reasoning chains that exceed the intrinsic representational capacity of sub-3B models. Consequently, during the exploration phase of GRPO, the model rarely generates a correct reasoning trace for these problems, leading to a sparse reward landscape. Instead of providing a directional gradient toward better reasoning, these hard samples likely contribute high-variance gradients that act as noise, potentially destabilizing the learning of simpler concepts. This explains why removing them and focusing on the ”learnable” signal from lower-difficulty tiers maintains performance while using ∼ 45% the training steps. 

Limitations We acknowledge several constraints in our study. First, we employed LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) Hu et al. (2021) rather than full fine-tuning. While our primary experiments rely on parameter-efficient fine-tuning via LoRA for computational tractability, we verified through a full fine-tuning ablation on the 0.5B model (detailed in Appendix F)) that the capacity boundary persists even with all parameters unfrozen. This confirms the saturation is an intrinsic property of the model’s representational scale rather than an optimization constraint. Second, our study is bounded by the scale of standard academic benchmarks (GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021), MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021)). The dynamics of difficulty scaling might differ in regimes with massive-scale synthetic data Gunasekar et al. (2023) (e.g., millions of samples), where brute-force memorization could mimic reasoning. Finally, our findings are specific to the 0.5B – 3B parameter regime; these trends likely invert for frontier models (70B+), where capacity is sufficient to leverage complex training signals. 

# 6 CONCLUSION 

In this work, we challenge the prevailing assumption that ”harder is better” for post-training align-ment.We demonstrate that for SLMs in resource-constrained settings, data difficulty is not a strict proxy for alignment quality. Our difficulty-stratified analysis reveals a sharp capacity boundary: models in the 0.5B–3B range saturate on complex reasoning tasks, rendering high-difficulty training samples computationally wasteful. Crucially, we show that training on only the lower-difficulty data matches full-dataset performance, offering a clear path to efficient alignment. Our findings suggest a resource-efficient alignment paradigm: train on what the model can learn from, not on what exceeds its capacity.

# A EXTENDED RELATED WORK 

Our research sits at the intersection of efficient language modeling, reinforcement learning for math-ematical reasoning, and data curriculum design. A.1 ALIGNMENT FOR REASONING 

Standard alignment techniques, such as Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) Ouyang et al. (2022), rely on a trained reward model and a value function (critic) to guide generation. While PPO has been fundamental in aligning models like LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), the memory overhead of maintaining a critic model often equal in size to the policy model is prohibitive for efficient training. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) circumvents this by optimizing the policy directly on offline preference pairs. However, for mathematical reasoning, where the solution space is deterministic (binary correctness) but the reasoning path is diverse, online exploration is crucial. We adopt Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024), which specifically targets this domain. GRPO samples a group of outputs for a single prompt and computes the baseline for the policy gradient from the group mean rather than a separate value function. This reduces memory usage and naturally incentivizes the model to outperform its own average generation, a dynamic well-suited for the iterative improvement of Chain-of-Thought reasoning. A.2 THE CAPACITY OF SMALL LANGUAGE MODELS (SLM S)While scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) have historically driven the development of massive models, recent focus has shifted toward efficiency. The emergence of SLMs, typically under 3B parameters, has demonstrated that high-quality data can offset parameter reduction. The “Phi” series (Gunasekar et al., 2023) validated that models trained on “textbook-quality” synthetic data could rival larger models. However, most SLM evaluations focus on Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). Our work extends this inquiry into the post-training phase, specifically investigating whether the reasoning capabilities of these compact models can be further elicited through reinforcement learning without inducing collapse. A.3 DATA COMPLEXITY AND CURRICULUM LEARNING 

The relationship between training data complexity and model performance is a subject of active debate. In mathematical reasoning, recent works argue for “upward” complexity scaling. Ding et al. (2025) and Parashar et al. (2025) propose that training on the frontier of difficulty aids in robust generalization. This aligns with findings in larger models where difficult samples provide the gradients necessary to refine complex decision boundaries. However, our findings suggest a “Capacity Boundary” for SLMs. This echoes the theoretical under-pinnings of “grokking” (Power et al., 2022), where certain reasoning behaviors only materialize past a scale threshold. We provide empirical evidence that for sub-3B models, the “hard” data widely advocated for in LLM literature functions as high-variance noise, leading to the negative transfer phenomena observed in our experiments.
